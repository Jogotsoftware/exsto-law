# Plain-Language Client Letters

## Purpose

Client letters are high-stakes documents — even routine ones. A missed appointment time, a garbled "what to bring," or jargon a client can't parse can derail a case. This skill handles two families:

- **Routine correspondence** — appointment confirmations, document requests, brief "we filed it" updates. Template-driven, checklist-gated.
- **Substantive status updates** — fuller case-status summaries, audience-matched to the client, the attorney's own notes, or a court.

**What this skill does NOT do:** decide what to tell the client about case strategy, legal merits, or bad news. Those are attorney judgment calls. Provide the relevant case facts in context and the attorney directs the framing. This skill structures and drafts what the attorney has decided to communicate.

**Every output is a draft for attorney review, not legal advice and not a legal opinion. The attorney owns the legal conclusion. Nothing produced here is sent, filed, or relied on until the attorney reviews, edits, and signs off.**

---

## Privilege and confidentiality

Letters referencing case facts, strategy, or client communications may be privileged. The draft inherits the source material's privilege status. Do not suggest distributing it beyond the privilege circle. Keep client letters in the matter file.

---

## Context grounding

If a matter or client is loaded in your context, ground all facts in it — use the client's name, matter details, and any relevant deadlines or events from context. If no matter is in context, ask the attorney which matter this letter belongs to before drafting. If the attorney has stated firm positions or preferences (tone, contact info, standard sign-off language) in context, apply them; if a position is not given, ask one short question or use a conservative default and flag the assumption explicitly.

**Jurisdiction default:** North Carolina. Surface this assumption if it affects the sign-off form or any legal reference in the letter. If the matter is in a different jurisdiction, adjust and flag.

## Letter type: Routine Correspondence

Use for appointments, document requests, and brief procedural updates. Not substantive advice.

### Appointment confirmation

```
Dear [Client name],

This confirms your appointment with Pacheco Law:

Date: [date]
Time: [time]
Where: [address / phone number for a call]
With: [attorney or staff name]

Please bring: [documents needed — ask attorney to confirm if not specified]

If you need to reschedule, please call us at [firm phone] at least 24 hours before your appointment.

[Signature block]
```

### Document request

```
Dear [Client name],

To move your case forward, we need the following documents from you:

- [Document 1 — describe plainly, e.g., "Your signed lease agreement"]
- [Document 2 — e.g., "The letter you received from the other party dated [date]"]
- [Document 3]

How to send them: [drop off / email to [address] / bring to next appointment]

Please send by: [date — if there is a deadline, say why in plain terms: "We need these by [date] so we can respond before the court's deadline."]

If you don't have some of these or aren't sure what we mean, call us at [firm phone] and we can help.

[Signature block]
```

### Brief procedural update

For "we filed it" / "we're waiting" updates only. For fuller substantive updates, use the status-update templates below.

```
Dear [Client name],

Quick update on your case: [one plain-English sentence — "We filed your response with the court on [date]" / "We sent the letter to the other party on [date]"].

What's next: [one sentence — "We're waiting for their response" / "The court will let us know the hearing date"].

You don't need to do anything right now. We'll reach out when we do.

[Signature block]
```

## Letter type: Substantive Status Update

Use when the attorney wants to give the client a fuller picture of where the case stands, or to produce an internal progress note, or a court-ready status report.

### Client-facing status update

**Reader:** The client. Plain language. Reading level target: 6th grade. No jargon. No legal analysis. No case weaknesses unless the attorney has decided it is time to have that conversation — and that is the attorney's decision, not this skill's.

**Include:**
- What has happened since the client last heard from the firm
- What is happening next and when
- What (if anything) the client needs to do
- How to reach the firm

**Do not include:** legal analysis, weaknesses in the case, strategic considerations, jargon.

If a legal term is unavoidable, explain it in plain English on first use: "We filed your 'answer' — that is the document that tells the court your side of the story."

```
Dear [Client name],

I wanted to update you on your case.

What has happened: [Plain English. "We filed your answer with the court on [date]" — not "The responsive pleading was submitted."]

What is next: [What and when, specifically. "The court scheduled a hearing for [date] at [time]. You need to be there." Or: "We are waiting for the other side's lawyer to respond. That usually takes a few weeks."]

What you need to do: [Specific and clear. Or: "Nothing right now — we will reach out when we need something from you."]

How to reach us: [Firm phone, email, hours]

[Signature block]
```

Before presenting this draft to the attorney, flag:
- Any jargon that slipped through
- Any statements that go beyond pure status into advice or assessment
- Any deadline or date that should be double-checked against the matter record

### Internal status note (attorney's own use)

**Reader:** The attorney, for their own case notes or a colleague. Knows the law. Wants to know where the case stands and what is needed next.

**Include:**
- Procedural posture
- What has been done since the last update
- Upcoming deadlines and hearings
- Issues that need a decision
- Current assessment

```markdown
# Status: [Client] — [Matter] — [Date]

**Procedural posture:** [pre-filing / answer filed / discovery / motion pending / etc.]

## Since last update

- [What has been done]

## Upcoming

| Date | Event | Action needed by |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| [date] | [deadline / hearing] | [date action needed] |

## Needs decision

- [Question or decision point — specific]

## Assessment

[How it is going. Strengths, concerns, strategic questions.]
```

Flag the assessment section as requiring the attorney's own thinking — the draft can scaffold it based on facts in context, but the attorney should revise it to reflect their actual judgment.

### Court-ready status report

**Reader:** A judge or clerk. Formal. Jurisdiction-specific. For use when a court has ordered a status report or one is required before a status conference.

**Format:** Per North Carolina local rules for the relevant court (flag the assumption; adjust if a different jurisdiction). Caption, procedural history, current status, outstanding matters, proposed next steps, signature block, certificate of service if filing.

Present the draft with a clear header:

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  DRAFT — Attorney review required before filing.
  Court filings are never submitted without attorney review.
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

[Caption — VERIFY against current local rules for this court]

STATUS REPORT

[Party] respectfully submits this status report pursuant to [the court's
order of [date] / Local Rule [X] / in advance of the status conference
scheduled for [date]].

1. Procedural history: [brief]

2. Current status: [discovery status / motion status / settlement status]

3. Outstanding matters: [what is pending]

4. Proposed next steps: [scheduling or next hearing, if the court wants input]

[Signature block — attorney name, firm, bar number, contact]

[Certificate of service — if filing]

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
VERIFY before filing:
- Caption format matches current local rules
- Status report requirements per standing orders for this judge
- Service requirements
- All [bracketed] fields are filled
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Plain-language check (apply to all letter types)

Before presenting any draft:

1. **Sentences:** Short. One idea per sentence. If a sentence runs past 25 words, split it.
2. **Jargon:** Every legal term gets a plain-English explanation in parentheses on first use, or is replaced entirely.
3. **Active voice:** "We filed your answer" not "Your answer was filed."
4. **Next step clarity:** The client should know exactly what they need to do (or that they need to do nothing) after reading the letter.
5. **Contact info:** Every client-facing letter ends with how to reach the firm.

---

## Before sending: required gates

Sending a letter to a client is a consequential act that may constitute legal advice or communication on the client's behalf. Apply these gates before presenting the draft as ready to send:

1. The attorney has reviewed, edited, and approved the draft.
2. All internal draft labels, `[VERIFY]` tags, and bracketed placeholder fields have been removed from the client-facing copy.
3. The signature block is accurate and complete.
4. If the letter references a deadline, date, or filed document — that fact has been confirmed against the matter record.

**This skill produces drafts. The attorney decides what goes out, edits accordingly, and is responsible for the final communication.**

---

## What this skill does NOT do

- **Substantive legal advice.** If the letter would say "here is what I think about your case" or "here is what you should do," that is not a status update — it is legal advice, and the attorney frames it, not this skill.
- **Bad news without attorney direction.** Case closing, adverse rulings, inability to help — those need attorney judgment before drafting. If the attorney describes the situation, this skill can help draft the language; the attorney decides what to say.
- **Anything to opposing counsel or a court without attorney review.** Opposing-counsel letters and court filings are attorney work product. Present the draft; the attorney reviews and sends or files.
- **Replace the attorney's assessment.** The internal status note's assessment section is the attorney's thinking. The draft can scaffold from facts in context, but the attorney owns the judgment.
