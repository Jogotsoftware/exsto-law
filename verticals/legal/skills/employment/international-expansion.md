# International Expansion Hiring Framework

> **Every output produced by this skill is a draft for attorney review — not legal advice and not a legal opinion.** The attorney owns all legal conclusions. Nothing here substitutes for outside employment counsel in the relevant country.

## Purpose

International hiring is frequently mishandled because no single function owns the full picture. Legal knows the employment-law questions but not the permanent-establishment (PE) risk. Finance knows the cost model but not the employee-representation triggers. HR knows the comp benchmarks but not the Day 1 compliance requirements.

This skill maps the terrain, drafts the right questions for each stakeholder, and produces a structured outside-counsel briefing request. It does not replace any of those functions.

**Scope:** This skill assumes the expansion is decided. It is not a "should we expand?" framework. It does not contain country-specific employment law — substantive local rules change frequently and vary by role, headcount, and industry. Every country is routed through an outside-counsel briefing rather than a stored reference table.

**Jurisdiction default:** Where no jurisdiction is specified, this skill defaults to United States federal law and North Carolina state law for the domestic side of the analysis. Surface this assumption explicitly and ask the attorney to confirm or correct it.

**Matter/client grounding:** If a matter or client is in context, ground the analysis in it throughout. If none is in context, ask the attorney which client or matter this is for before proceeding.

---

## Step 1 — Information Gathering

Ask all of the following in a single block before proceeding:

> Before I build the expansion plan I need to understand the shape of this expansion. Please answer what you can — gaps are themselves useful data:
>
> **The expansion**
> - Which country?
> - What roles are being hired? (Job function matters — a sales rep closing deals creates different legal exposure than an engineer writing code.)
> - How many hires are planned in the next 12 months?
> - When does the first person need to start?
>
> **Current state**
> - Is there already a legal entity in this country?
> - Has an Employer of Record (EOR) provider been used or considered?
> - Has tax or finance been looped in yet?
> - Is there outside employment counsel in this country?
>
> **Strategic context**
> - Is this a long-term strategic commitment (building a real team) or a market test (one or two hires, see how it goes)?
> - Who is the executive sponsor making the structure decision?

Wait for answers before proceeding to Step 2.

---

## Step 2 — Employer of Record vs. Entity Framing

Do not make this decision. Frame it with enough precision that the CFO and tax counsel can make it. Present the following structured framing, tailored to the intake answers.

**Core trade-off:**

| Factor | Points toward EOR | Points toward Entity |
|---|---|---|
| Headcount in 12 months | Fewer hires | More hires |
| Timeline to first hire | Short runway | Longer runway available |
| Strategic commitment | Testing the market | Long-term presence |
| Cost sensitivity | EOR markup acceptable | Scale makes entity more efficient |
| Control needs | Low — EOR employer handles local HR | High — want direct employer relationship |
| Intellectual property sensitivity | Lower | Higher — entity ownership is cleaner |

Specific headcount break-even points, EOR markup ranges, entity setup costs, and timelines vary by country and provider. Do not estimate these. Route those questions to tax/finance and the EOR provider.

**Permanent establishment (PE) risk — flag if applicable:**

If the roles include sales, business development, account management, or anyone with authority to negotiate or sign contracts on behalf of the client, flag this explicitly before any other analysis:

> **PE Risk:** [Role type] may create a taxable permanent establishment in [country] even before a legal entity exists. This is a tax question, not an employment question. Tax counsel must assess PE risk before the first hire.

**Questions to surface for the CFO and tax counsel:**

> - At [N] hires over 12 months, at what headcount does entity setup become more cost-effective than EOR (accounting for EOR markup, setup costs, and ongoing compliance burden)?
> - [If PE-risk roles:] Do these role types create a taxable permanent establishment in [country]? If yes, does that change the entity timeline?
> - If the client starts with EOR and converts to entity later, what are the transition risks for employees already on the EOR?
> - Who is the preferred EOR provider for this country, and has their local compliance track record been vetted?

Present this framing in chat for the attorney to review. Flag any assumptions you made about the intake answers.

## Step 3 — Cross-Functional Triggers

For each function that needs to be engaged, state what they need to do and draft the specific questions legal should ask them. Do not just say "loop in finance" — produce the ask.

**Tax counsel** (always required before the first hire)

What they need to do: PE risk analysis, determination of whether an entity is required for tax purposes, advice on equity tax treatment in this jurisdiction.

Draft questions for legal to ask tax counsel:
- Does hiring a [role type] in [country] create a permanent establishment or taxable nexus before an entity is in place?
- What is the exposure window if hiring begins before the PE question is resolved?
- How are equity awards (RSUs/options) taxed in [country]? Does the client need local tax counsel to advise employees at grant and vesting?
- If an entity is set up, what intercompany services agreement is needed between the subsidiary and the US parent?

**Finance / Payroll** (required before the first paycheck)

What they need to do: identify a local payroll provider (or confirm the EOR handles it), budget mandatory employer contributions, set up local banking if using an entity.

Draft questions for legal to ask finance:
- Has a local payroll provider been identified? (If EOR: confirm the EOR handles payroll including local social contributions.)
- What are the mandatory employer contributions in [country] — pension, social insurance, healthcare — and are these budgeted in the compensation model?
- How will equity grants be administered for employees in [country]? Has anyone modeled the employer-side tax withholding obligations at vesting?

**HR / Total Rewards** (required before an offer is made)

What they need to do: benefits benchmarking, compensation benchmarking against local market, confirmation of mandatory vs. supplemental benefits.

Draft questions for legal to ask HR:
- What benefits are legally mandatory in [country] vs. market-standard? (Avoid accidentally promising more than required or less than market.)
- Is the client's standard equity package competitive in this market, or does local practice differ significantly?
- Who will be the employee's day-to-day manager — local or remote from headquarters? (This can affect employee-representation analysis and employment agreement terms in some jurisdictions.)

**Outside counsel** (required — do not skip)

What they need to do: research and advise on the local employment framework for these roles and headcount, review and draft the local employment agreement, flag structural issues with the proposed arrangement.

The briefing request in Step 4 is the agenda for this engagement. It should be sent at the start of the engagement, not piecemeal.

## Step 4 — Outside-Counsel Briefing Request

Draft the following briefing request, tailored to the intake answers, for the attorney to send to local outside counsel. Present it in chat for review.

> **Outside counsel briefing request — [Country]**
>
> We are planning to hire [N] employees in [Country] starting [date], in the following roles: [roles]. Target headcount over 12 months: [N]. Preferred structure (subject to your advice and tax counsel): [EOR / entity / undecided]. We need a briefing covering each of the following. Please answer as questions with citations to primary law, not as a reference table — we want to track changes over time.
>
> 1. **Entity and engagement structure** — What are the options (direct hire via entity, EOR, contractor) and what are the practical and legal trade-offs for this headcount and these roles?
>
> 2. **Employment contract requirements** — What form is required or standard? What must be included? What cannot be included or is unenforceable? What language or translation requirements apply?
>
> 3. **Termination** — What are the notice requirements and severance obligations? How difficult is termination in practice (protected-cause standards, social-selection rules in reductions in force, reasonable-notice common-law exposure)? What documentation standard should be established from day one?
>
> 4. **Mandatory benefits and employer contributions** — What must be provided by law (pension, social insurance, healthcare, paid leave, bonuses)? What are the current employer contribution rates to budget? Please cite the controlling statute and verify currency.
>
> 5. **Restrictive covenants** — Are non-competes enforceable? Under what conditions and with what compensation requirements? What confidentiality and intellectual property assignment language holds up?
>
> 6. **Employee representation** — Are there works council, employee representation, union, or collective bargaining requirements? At what headcount do they trigger? What consultation or co-determination rights apply? Are we covered by any sectoral collective agreement even if we are not unionized?
>
> 7. **Data protection** — What obligations apply to employee data? Is there a data transfer mechanism needed for employee data flowing to the United States?
>
> 8. **Work authorization** — What permits or visas are required for foreign nationals? What are the processing timelines?
>
> 9. **Industry-specific rules** — Are there sector rules, awards, or collective agreements that apply to our industry regardless of whether we are unionized?
>
> 10. **Contractor / independent-contractor risk** — What is the country's test for worker classification, and what are the deemed-employment or reclassification risks for any contractor arrangements under consideration?
>
> 11. **Equity / incentive compensation** — Are there local tax, securities, or employment-law rules governing how RSUs, options, or other equity are granted here?
>
> 12. **Day 1 compliance** — What must be in place before the first employee starts? Registration requirements, notices, filings, required postings?
>
> 13. **Top 2–3 surprises for US companies hiring here for the first time** — What do you wish clients had asked earlier? What has changed recently that a US team might not have caught?

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- Advise on specific local employment law — that is outside counsel's job.
- Make the EOR vs. entity decision — it frames the decision for the right decision-makers.
- Draft the local employment agreement — flags that outside counsel must do this.
- State country-specific rules from its own knowledge — every country is routed through an outside-counsel briefing.
- Substitute for outside-counsel engagement — every new country requires local counsel, no exceptions.
- Search for current statutory rates, contribution percentages, or notice periods — if the attorney wants current figures, use web_search and note the retrieval date and source; treat all figures as needing verification by outside counsel.
