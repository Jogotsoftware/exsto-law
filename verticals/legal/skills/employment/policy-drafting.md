## Purpose

A policy that's right for California may be wrong—or unnecessary—in North Carolina. This skill drafts a clear core policy and generates state supplements wherever the employer's jurisdictional footprint requires different rules.

**Default jurisdiction assumption:** North Carolina / federal US law, unless the attorney specifies a different footprint. Surface this assumption explicitly in every output.

---

## Every output is a draft for attorney review — not a policy in effect

Publishing a handbook policy has legal consequences. In several states a handbook can bind the employer as a contractual promise, and wage, leave, and accommodation policies are routinely read against the employer. A licensed attorney reviews, edits as needed, and takes professional responsibility before the policy is rolled out. Do not advise the attorney to publish or distribute this draft unreviewed.

---

## Step 1 — Scope the policy

Ask (or resolve from context) before drafting:

- **Topic** — What is the policy about? (Remote work, parental leave, social media use, expense reimbursement, etc.)
- **Reason** — Why now? (Legal requirement, incident, business growth, noticed gap)
- **Applicability** — Who does it apply to? (All employees, certain roles, certain locations)
- **Jurisdictional footprint** — Which states or countries does the employer operate in? If not provided, ask the attorney one short question: "Which states do your employees work in?" Default to North Carolina only and flag that assumption if no answer is given.

---

## Step 2 — Jurisdictional scan

For each state in the footprint, check whether that jurisdiction has a specific rule on this topic.

**Common topics with jurisdictional variance (non-exhaustive):**

| Topic | Variance |
|---|---|
| Paid sick / family leave | State mandates (CA, NY, CO, WA, CT, MA, NJ, OR, WA, MD, IL, and growing list) with different accrual rates, permitted uses, and carryover rules |
| Parental leave | State paid-leave programs layer on top of federal FMLA (CA PFL, NY PFL, WA PFML, etc.) |
| Meal and rest breaks | California requires penalty pay for missed breaks; most states are minimal; North Carolina follows federal minimums |
| Expense reimbursement | California requires reimbursement of all necessary business expenses; most states (including NC) do not have an equivalent statutory mandate |
| Pay transparency / salary range disclosure | Growing list of states and localities requiring pay ranges in job postings (CO, CA, NY, WA, IL, others) |
| Non-compete agreements | Unenforceable or heavily restricted in CA, ND, MN, OK, and others; NC enforces with reasonable limits; see hiring-review skill |
| Final pay timing | Varies widely by state (NC requires next regular payday, with exceptions) |
| Drug testing | State-by-state variation on permissible testing, notice, and off-duty marijuana use |

If the topic has no meaningful jurisdictional variance (dress code, acknowledgment process, etc.), skip this step and note that in the drafting notes.

---

## Step 3 — Draft the core policy

One policy. Applies to all employees in all locations. Written so employees can understand it without a lawyer.

**Structure:**

```markdown
**Purpose**
[One sentence — why this policy exists.]

**Scope**
[Who it applies to and when it takes effect.]

**Policy**
[What is required, permitted, or prohibited. Use plain language. Avoid "heretofore," "notwithstanding," and nested exceptions.]

**Process**
[How to make a request, who approves it, timelines, what documentation is needed.]

**Consequences**
[What happens if the policy is not followed — keep proportionate and consistent with the handbook's general discipline language.]

**Questions**
[Who to contact. For a solo/small firm context: the supervising attorney or firm administrator.]
```

**Cross-check before finalizing:**

- Does this policy conflict with any other policy the attorney has mentioned or that is in context?
- Does it promise more than the firm intends to deliver? A policy is a promise—courts hold employers to handbook promises.
- Does it inadvertently create an employment contract? Include "this policy is not a contract of employment and may be amended at any time" language if it is not already in the firm's standard disclaimer.
- Does it require an accommodation process (leave, disability, religion)? If so, flag that the firm needs a consistent interactive-process procedure.

---

## Step 4 — State supplements

For each jurisdiction in the footprint where the rule differs from the core policy, draft a tight supplement. Only state what is different — do not repeat the core.

```markdown
### [State] Supplement

Employees working in [State] are subject to the following [in addition to / instead of] the core policy:

- [Specific difference and brief reason]
- [Cite the governing statute or regulation where helpful, e.g., Cal. Lab. Code § 226.7]
```

If the firm's footprint is North Carolina only, state clearly: "No state supplement is required — North Carolina does not impose requirements beyond the core policy on this topic" (if true), or note the NC-specific rule if one applies.

---

## Step 5 — Firm positions and context

Apply any firm-specific positions or preferences the attorney has stated in this conversation or in context (e.g., the firm's existing handbook language, tone preferences, benefit levels already in place). If a material policy choice is not addressed — such as carryover caps, approval authority, or progressive discipline steps — do one of the following:

- Ask the attorney one short, specific question to resolve the gap, or
- Use a conservative default (the choice least likely to bind the firm to unintended obligations) and flag the assumption explicitly.

Never invent firm-specific positions as authoritative. Never assume the firm matches a larger employer's practice.

## What this skill does not do

- **Approve the policy.** It drafts; the attorney approves and owns the legal conclusion.
- **Roll out the policy.** Communication to employees and acknowledgment collection are HR workflow steps outside this skill.
- **Cover every jurisdiction.** Only the states the attorney identifies. If the footprint expands, ask the attorney to re-invoke the skill with the updated list.
- **Substitute for jurisdiction-specific counsel.** For states with complex leave or wage regimes (California, New York, Washington), flag that local employment counsel review is advisable before rollout.
- **Access external legal databases.** This assistant uses web_search and any documents or sources the attorney provides. It cannot query Westlaw, run a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction statutory comparison service, or access the firm's existing handbook unless the attorney pastes or uploads it. Flag this limit where the accuracy of the jurisdictional scan is material.
