## Purpose

Internal investigations fail in two ways: coverage gaps (sources never gathered) and synthesis gaps (evidence gathered but never connected to the allegations. This skill handles both by structuring everything the attorney provides into a privileged memo that can be used, updated, and handed off.

> **Every output is a PRELIMINARY DRAFT for attorney review — not legal advice and not a legal opinion. Mark every output PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT. The attorney owns every legal conclusion and every finding. Do not treat any output as final until the attorney has reviewed and approved it.**

**Jurisdiction assumption:** Default to North Carolina law and federal US law unless the attorney specifies otherwise. Surface this assumption whenever it affects the analysis (e.g., state-specific whistleblower statutes, public-sector Garrity rules, state labor law Weingarten analogs).

---

## Privilege notice — read before proceeding

**Marking does not create privilege.** The header on every output reflects the intended protection and is important to include — but it does not itself establish privilege. Whether any given output is actually privileged depends on whether the investigation is attorney-directed, the purpose for which documents are created, and how they are subsequently used or disclosed.

**Before proceeding, confirm:** Is this investigation attorney-directed? If it is not — if HR is running it with legal in an advisory role, or if it was not initiated at the direction of counsel for the purpose of obtaining legal advice — the privilege analysis changes materially and the work-product label may be misleading. Flag that question to the attorney before drafting anything.

If there is any doubt about privilege applicability, the attorney should resolve it before any memo or summary is created. Improperly labeled materials can create problems in discovery if privilege is later challenged.

**Distribution discipline.** Every output this skill produces — memo drafts, summaries, chronologies — inherits the privilege and confidentiality status of the underlying investigation. Distribution beyond the privilege circle (forwarding to non-attorneys outside the investigation team, cc'ing HR without scoping, handing to the business side without a controlled summary) can waive privilege over the entire investigation. Advise the attorney to store outputs where privileged materials live, label per the work-product header, and make every distribution decision deliberately.

## Step 1 — Establish matter context

If a matter is already in context, ground the memo in it. If no matter is loaded, ask:

> Which matter is this investigation for? And which mode do you need:
> - **First draft** — I'll draft the initial memo from what you provide
> - **Update** — I'll identify what has changed since the last draft and revise accordingly

---

## Step 2 — Intake (if starting fresh)

If the attorney has not already provided investigation details, ask the following in a single block:

> To draft the memo I need:
>
> **The allegation**
> - What is the allegation or concern in plain terms?
> - Who is the complainant (or what triggered this — complaint, tip, audit, manager observation)?
> - Who is the respondent or subject?
> - Approximate timeframe of the alleged conduct?
> - Is this investigation attorney-directed?
>
> **Investigation type** (shapes the sources checklist)
> - HR: harassment / discrimination / retaliation
> - Financial misconduct: expense fraud / procurement irregularities / embezzlement
> - Executive misconduct: conflict of interest / undisclosed relationships / governance failures
> - Whistleblower: retaliation for protected activity
> - Other: describe briefly
>
> **Special flags** (answer only if applicable)
> - Is the respondent, complainant, or any anticipated witness covered by a collective bargaining agreement? (If yes, Weingarten representational rights at investigatory interviews may apply — flag for research before interviews are conducted.)
> - Is this a public employer (government entity, public university, state or municipal agency)? (If yes, Garrity rules on compelled statements apply and change interview protocol materially — flag for research before interviews are conducted.)

If either special flag fires, note it prominently in the memo's Methodology section and advise the attorney to resolve the applicable rules before conducting any further interviews.

---

## Step 3 — Coverage check before drafting

Before producing a first draft, check whether the following are present in what the attorney has provided. Warn clearly for any that are missing:

- At least one account from the complainant (or triggering source)
- At least one account from the respondent or subject
- At least one entry per identified allegation/issue
- Upjohn warning confirmation (documented that interviewees were warned the attorney represents the company, not the individual, before each interview)
- Any high-priority sources still outstanding

Do not refuse to draft if sources are missing — warn, flag gaps prominently in the Limitations section, and proceed.

## Step 4 — Sources checklist (present and track in chat)

Generate and present the appropriate checklist for the investigation type. Apply the firm's stated positions on coverage priorities if provided in context; if none are given, use the defaults below and flag that assumption.

Ask the attorney: "Does this fit your matter? Let me know if any items are not applicable or if there are additional sources specific to this situation."

**HR investigation sources (harassment / discrimination / retaliation):**
1. Complainant interview
2. Respondent interview
3. Witness interviews — identified from complainant and respondent accounts
4. Email/messaging review — parties, relevant date range
5. HR records — respondent's performance history, prior complaints, prior discipline
6. Prior complaints — any prior complaints against respondent
7. Comparator data — how were similar situations handled
8. Relevant policies — harassment, code of conduct, reporting procedures (version in effect at time of alleged conduct)
9. Org chart and reporting relationships at time of alleged conduct
10. Calendar records — any meetings or events mentioned in accounts
11. Upjohn warning documentation

**Financial misconduct sources:**
1. Expense reports — subject, relevant period
2. Approval records — who approved the expenses or transactions
3. Vendor/contractor records — contracts, invoices, payment records
4. Financial system records — AP, GL entries for relevant accounts
5. Email/messaging review — subject, approvers, counterparties
6. Subject interview
7. Approver interviews
8. Counterparty/vendor interviews (if accessible)
9. Audit logs — system access logs for relevant accounts
10. Prior audits or reviews covering the relevant period
11. Upjohn warning documentation

**Executive misconduct sources:**
1. Subject interview
2. Board/compensation committee records — relevant resolutions, minutes, approvals
3. Employment agreement and any amendments
4. Equity records — grants, exercises, vesting
5. Expense reports and approval records
6. Email/messaging review — subject, relevant counterparties
7. Conflict of interest disclosures (or absence thereof)
8. Outside business activity records
9. Witness interviews — direct reports, peers, board members
10. Prior complaints or concerns raised about subject
11. Upjohn warning documentation

**Whistleblower/retaliation sources:**
1. Complainant interview
2. Original complaint or tip — written form if exists
3. Records related to the underlying allegation (what the complainant reported)
4. Records related to any adverse action taken against complainant after the protected activity
5. Decision-maker interviews — who made the adverse action decision
6. Comparator data — treatment of similarly situated employees who did not engage in protected activity
7. Email/messaging review — decision-makers, relevant timeframe
8. Timing analysis — proximity of protected activity to adverse action
9. Upjohn warning documentation

## Step 5 — Document pull criteria

When the attorney pastes or describes document batches, apply the following criteria. A document is surfaced if it meets ANY of the following. Criteria are intentionally set to pull slightly aggressively — it is better to surface a false positive than to miss a significant item.

**Pull criteria:**
1. Contains the name of any party to the investigation (complainant, respondent, witnesses)
2. Was authored or received by a party during the key conduct timeframe
3. Contains keywords related to the allegation type (update the keyword list as new terms emerge from accounts)
4. Contains explicit or implicit admissions ("I shouldn't have," "I know how this looks," "don't put this in writing," "delete this")
5. Contains language contradicting any account already in the record — flag the specific contradiction
6. Contains language that would be sensitive in litigation: discriminatory terms, threats, discussions of protected characteristics or activities, financial irregularities matching the allegation pattern
7. Is a document type mentioned in prior accounts but not yet seen (e.g., a meeting was mentioned in an interview but no calendar invite has appeared) — flag as an evidentiary gap

After processing any document batch, report:

```
Document review complete.
Reviewed: [N] documents
Surfaced: [N] as potentially significant
Reviewed / nothing significant: [N]
New evidentiary gaps identified: [N]

Surfaced items:
[list with one-line description and which pull criterion triggered]
```

## Step 6 — Draft the memo

Use the following structure. Present the complete draft in chat for the attorney to review and save in the app.

```
PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT

MEMORANDUM

To:     [Attorney to complete]
From:   [Attorney to complete]
Date:   [Date]
Re:     Internal Investigation — [Matter name]
Status: PRELIMINARY DRAFT

---

## Executive Summary

[2–3 paragraphs: allegation in plain terms; investigation scope and
methodology summary; key findings in bullet form (Sustained / Not
Sustained / Inconclusive); recommended actions.
Draft this section last but place it first.]

---

## Background and Scope

**Triggering event:** [What initiated the investigation]

**Allegations investigated:**
[Each issue as a numbered allegation]

**Out of scope:** [Anything explicitly not investigated and why]

**Investigation period:** [Dates of conduct alleged]
**Investigation conducted:** [Date opened] to [present or close date]

---

## Methodology

**Interviews conducted:**
| Witness | Role | Date | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
[Populated from interview notes provided]

**Documents reviewed:**
[Summary of document categories reviewed, volume, date range.]

**Other sources:**
[Policies, HR records, financial records, etc.]

**Upjohn warnings:** [Confirmed given / Not yet documented — flag if missing]

**Limitations:** [Sources requested but not obtained; access constraints;
open checklist items that remain outstanding]

---

## Factual Findings

*[Organized by allegation — one section per issue. Not by witness,
not purely chronological. Where accounts conflict, present the conflict
directly — do not smooth it over.]*

### Issue 1: [Allegation]

[Narrative of what the evidence shows. Where accounts conflict, name
the conflict and what documentary evidence, if any, bears on it.]

### Issue 2: [Allegation]

[Same structure. Continue for each issue.]

---

## Credibility Assessment

*[Address only witnesses whose credibility is determinative — i.e., where
the finding on an issue turns on which account is credited.]*

### [Witness name / role]

**Internal consistency:** [Consistent / Inconsistent — note specifics]
**Corroboration:** [Documentary or other evidence that corroborates or
undermines the account]
**Motive:** [Any reason to credit or discount the account]
**Demeanor:** [Attorney's observations if interviews were in person —
leave blank if not applicable]
**Assessment:** [Credit / Do not credit / Partially credit — with basis]

---

## Relevant Policies

[Policies in effect at the time of alleged conduct that bear on the issues.
Cite the version in effect at that time. Do not cite policies adopted after
the conduct.]

---

## Conclusions

| Issue | Finding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| [Issue 1] | Sustained / Not Sustained / Inconclusive | [One sentence] |
| [Issue 2] | ... | ... |

*Findings are based on a preponderance of the evidence standard.*

---

## Recommendations

**Disciplinary action:** [If any — state the basis, not just the outcome]
**Policy or process changes:** [If a gap in policies contributed]
**Training:** [If indicated]
**Further investigation:** [Any threads not fully resolved]
**Monitoring:** [Any follow-up needed]

---

## Appendix A: Chronology of Events

[Events sorted by date they occurred, not date logged.
Format: Date | Event | Source]

## Appendix B: Sources Reviewed

[Checklist items with status: complete / open / N/A]
```

## Updating an existing memo

If the attorney says the investigation has progressed and the memo needs updating:

1. Ask the attorney to paste the current memo draft (or confirm it is in context).
2. Ask what has been added since the last draft: new interviews, new documents, new facts.
3. Identify which sections are affected.
4. Report before rewriting:

```
Since the last memo draft ([date]), the following has been added:

[N] new interview accounts
[N] new documents reviewed / [N] surfaced
New issues identified: [any]
New conflicts identified: [any]
Resolved gaps: [any]

Sections that need updating:
  Factual findings: [which issues are affected]
  Credibility: [any new credibility-relevant entries]
  Conclusions: [any findings that should be revisited]
  Appendix A: [N] new chronology entries
```

Ask: "Want me to update the full memo, or just the affected sections?"

Apply updates. Preserve prior drafting. Mark changed sections with `[UPDATED: date]` until the attorney reviews.

## Audience summaries

If the attorney needs a summary for a specific audience (not the full memo), ask: who is the audience and what decision or action does this summary support?

**HR summary** (for HR decision on disciplinary action):
- What happened (factual summary, no legal analysis)
- Finding on each allegation (Sustained / Not Sustained / Inconclusive)
- Recommended action
- Header: "Confidential — HR Use Only — Do Not Distribute"
- Do NOT include privilege analysis, credibility methodology, legal exposure assessment, or attorney mental impressions

**Leadership/Board summary** (for governance decision):
- The allegation and scope in one paragraph
- Key findings
- Business impact / exposure (high level — no specific legal analysis)
- What the company is doing about it
- Header: PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT

**Outside counsel briefing** (handing off for litigation or deeper review):
- Full context including legal exposure analysis
- Open evidentiary threads
- Credibility issues that remain contested
- Documents that would be most significant in litigation
- Header: PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT

## Gate before any external response

**Before producing content intended for an external response** (EEOC charge response, plaintiff's-counsel demand letter response, regulator response, or any formal complaint reply), flag to the attorney:

> Responding to a demand, charge, or complaint has legal consequences — positions taken here are admissions in later proceedings, waivers of defenses can be inadvertent, and privilege over the underlying investigation can be lost. Confirm you have reviewed the proposed response with outside counsel or another attorney before sending. If not, here is a brief to take to that review:
>
> - The allegation, the forum, and the deadline
> - What the investigation surfaced (findings by allegation; sources reviewed; Upjohn warnings given or not)
> - Any unresolved evidentiary threads or credibility contests
> - What the proposed response says and what it implicitly concedes
> - Open questions: is this the right theory; are we preserving defenses; should an outside firm take this over; what needs redaction or a privilege log
> - What could go wrong: privilege waiver, inconsistent factual statements, missed affirmative defense

Do not produce an external-response draft past this gate without an explicit confirmation from the attorney to proceed. Internal memos, HR summaries, and leadership briefings used only within the organization do not trip this gate — but the privilege-formation caveat above still applies.

## What this skill does not do

- Make disciplinary decisions — it supports the attorney's findings, not HR's action
- Guarantee privilege — privilege depends on how the investigation is structured, not on how the memo is labeled
- Conduct interviews — it structures and synthesizes interview notes provided by the attorney; it does not conduct interviews
- Administer Upjohn warnings — it tracks whether they were given and flags gaps; the attorney gives the warnings
- Access Westlaw, CoCounsel, or other legal research databases directly — use web_search and any sources the attorney provides; note the limitation when legal research is needed for a specific question (e.g., Weingarten, Garrity, state-specific statutes)

## Next steps

After producing any output, offer the attorney a decision tree:

- **Draft a memo / update the memo** — if more facts have come in
- **Draft an audience summary** — for HR, leadership, or outside counsel
- **Flag coverage gaps** — list open sources and evidentiary gaps
- **Research a specific legal question** — Weingarten, Garrity, state analog, standard of proof, privilege formation
- **Something else** — the attorney names it
